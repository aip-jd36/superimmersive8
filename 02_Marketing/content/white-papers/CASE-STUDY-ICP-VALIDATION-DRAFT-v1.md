## Introduction: Building in a Category That Doesn't Exist Yet

There is no established playbook for building a compliance product in a category that didn't exist 18 months ago.

When AI video tools — Runway, Kling, Pika, Sora — began producing commercially viable output in late 2024, a gap opened in the market that nobody had named yet. Production agencies were starting to deliver AI-generated video to brand clients. Brand legal teams were starting to ask questions nobody had answers to. The question wasn't "is this good?" It was "where did this come from, who owns it, and what are we exposed to if we use it?"

That gap — between AI video being commercially produced and AI video being commercially safe to use — is where SuperImmersive 8 was built. But building into a gap no one has mapped means your first hypothesis about who your customer is, what they need, and how they'll pay for it is probably wrong. Ours was wrong three times before we got it right.

This is the story of how we got it wrong, what told us we were wrong, and how 144 LinkedIn replies ultimately validated an ICP we couldn't have written on day one.

---

## Version 1: The Supply-Side Trap

**January 2026.**

The original thesis was simple: AI filmmakers were creating remarkable work with no distribution infrastructure. Platforms didn't know how to licence it. Brands didn't know how to find it. Build a representation agency — sign filmmakers, document rights, license to platforms and brands — and take 20% commission.

The model had logic. Filmmaker representation was a proven structure. AI video was an emerging asset class. The combination seemed obvious.

What it required, however, was that filmmakers would sign with an unproven agency before any buyers existed. That buyers would commit to licensing deals before any catalog existed. That both sides of a two-sided marketplace would trust a company with no track record, no sales history, and nothing to show except a deck.

Instagram outreach to AI filmmakers began in January 2026. The responses were warm. The conversations were genuine. The conversions were zero.

Every serious conversation hit the same wall. Not politely. Not aggressively. Just honestly:

*"Show me buyers first. Then I'll consider it."*

This was Essa, a Hong Kong-based AI filmmaker, one of the most talented people we spoke to in those early months. Leon, another serious prospect, said the same thing in different words. These weren't difficult people. They were making a rational decision. An unproven platform plus a 20% commission structure equals low expected value. They had other options for their work. We hadn't earned their trust yet.

At the time, this felt like a messaging problem. We wrote better pitches. We refined the value proposition. We tried different angles. None of it worked because the problem wasn't the message — it was the structure.

A two-sided marketplace deadlock cannot be solved with better copy. You cannot pitch your way out of a chicken-egg problem.

---

## Version 2: The Right Direction, With a Dangerous Word

**February 2026.**

The obvious pivot from supply-side failure was to flip the model: lead with buyer-facing messaging instead. Stop pitching filmmakers on distribution and start pitching agencies on access to rights-verified AI content they could legally use with brand clients.

The new framing: *"We give brands access to AI video that won't get you sued."*

This landed differently. Agency responses improved. The legal framing resonated. The EU AI Act was generating regulatory anxiety across European markets. UK agencies were starting to field questions from clients about AI disclosure requirements. Something in the v2 message was connecting with a genuine need.

But something else happened in February 2026 that mattered more than the messaging pivot.

We ran the business plan through two independent AI advisors — ChatGPT and Gemini — separately, with the same brief. Both came back with the same flag: **the words "rights-cleared" were legally dangerous.**

"Rights-cleared" implies a warranty. A legal guarantee that the content is safe to use. If an agency relied on "rights-cleared" language in a client contract and faced an IP dispute, the word "cleared" would appear in discovery. SI8 would have created liability it could never actually back up.

The fix was two words: replace "rights-cleared" with **"rights verified"** throughout every document, template, email, pitch, and website page.

The distinction matters precisely:

- **Rights-cleared** = "we guarantee this is legally safe" → assumes liability SI8 cannot sustain
- **Rights verified** = "we reviewed this against a documented process" → offers rigour without warranty

This was not a marketing decision. It was a liability decision. It reshaped the entire legal posture of the business and became the foundation that every subsequent version was built on.

Version 2 also established what would become a permanent strategic principle: **geographic focus over geographic ambition.** The initial instinct was to target Singapore, Manila, Kuala Lumpur, Bangkok, and Jakarta simultaneously. The revised plan selected Singapore first, proved the model there, then expanded.

## Version 3: The Right Product in the Wrong Business Model

**February–March 2026.**

By Version 3, the product was genuinely differentiated.

The insight: AI tools make something possible that traditional production never could. A brand could commission their product regenerated into an existing AI film — logo integrated, colour palette matched, product featured — without a reshoot. Not stock licensing. Not product placement negotiated on a set. Retroactive brand integration into an AI-generated world.

The positioning became: **SI8 as the "Bad Boy Indemnifier."** While Adobe's Content Credentials only cover Adobe Firefly — the safest, most conservative AI tool — SI8 covered Runway, Kling, Pika, and Sora. The tools that brand legal teams were genuinely afraid of. The competitive framing wrote itself: *"Adobe verifies Firefly only. We verify the tools your legal team is blocking."*

This was a strong position. Defensible. Differentiated. Pointing at a real market gap that a well-resourced competitor couldn't easily close because Adobe's incentive is to promote Firefly, not compete with it.

But Version 3 was still built on a broken structure.

The revenue model still required a catalog to exist before licensing deals could happen. The catalog required filmmakers to sign. Filmmakers signing required buyers to be visible. We had dressed the chicken-egg deadlock in much better clothes, but it was still there.

## The Two Calls That Broke Version 3

**March 2026.**

The education that ended Version 3 didn't come from a spreadsheet or a strategic review. It came from two conversations.

**The first call was with a production agency in the UK market.**

The pitch was the full Version 3 thesis: Rights Verified catalog, AI product placement, chain of title documentation, premium licensing deals for brand clients. The agency was engaged. They understood the compliance problem. They were asking the right questions.

Then: *"Can you show us some examples? What does the catalog look like right now?"*

There was no catalog. There were five films in various stages of development. There was a sample Chain of Title document for a fictional film. There were letters of intent from filmmakers who had expressed interest but not signed anything.

The agency was polite. They said they'd circle back when there was more to see. The call ended.

**The second call was with an agency in Singapore.**

The Singapore market had already shown low response rates — approximately 200 outreach contacts yielding under 5% engagement, significantly below what the London market was producing on the same pitch. But this was a warm lead. Someone had responded and wanted to understand what SI8 offered.

Same pitch. Same question: *"What do you have to show us right now?"*

Same answer.

**What these two calls revealed was not that the pitch was wrong. It was that the product didn't exist yet in the form the market needed to see it.**

Four independent market signals had now converged on the same message:

| Source | Signal |
|---|---|
| Essa (Hong Kong filmmaker) | "Show me buyers first, then I'll consider it" |
| Leon (filmmaker) | Same pattern — no conversion without demand proof |
| UK production agency | "Can you show us examples? What does the catalog look like?" |
| Singapore agency | "What do you have to show us right now?" |

And from two other buyer-side conversations:
- Isaac at Minted Creative: *"I need to see examples first."*
- The Media Shop: same requirement, different words.

Six people. Three markets. One message. The structure was the problem.

No sales process, better pitch deck, or sharper message could solve what was fundamentally a sequencing problem. You cannot show buyers a catalog that doesn't exist yet. You cannot build a catalog without filmmakers who are willing to sign. Filmmakers won't sign without seeing buyers. The loop was closed.

## The V4 Pivot: How the CarFax Model Unlocked the Deadlock

**Late February–March 2026.**

After the UK and Singapore calls, the business plan went back through independent peer review — ChatGPT and Gemini working separately on the same brief.

Both arrived at the same solution.

The diagnosis: every version of the business from v1 through v3 assumed the marketplace needed to exist before revenue could flow. This was the wrong assumption. The fix was to find a revenue stream that generated cash before the marketplace existed — and used that cash flow to build the marketplace as a byproduct, not a prerequisite.

The analogy they both reached independently: **CarFax.**

CarFax makes money from the vehicle history report. Not from selling cars. They don't need car dealers to be on the platform before they can charge buyers for information about a specific car. The report is the product. The report generates the revenue. The marketplace grows around the report over time.

Applied to SI8: production agencies and brands will pay **$499 for a Chain of Title verification** on AI video — a 90-minute human review producing a legally-structured document that establishes provenance, documents tool usage, identifies IP risk, and clears the content for commercial deployment. They pay upfront. SI8 delivers the document. Revenue on day one, with no catalog required.

After the verification is complete, the creator sees one checkbox: *"List this film in the SI8 Showcase for licensing opportunities?"*

If yes — the film appears in the public catalog with a Rights Verified badge. Buyers find it. Licenses happen. SI8 takes 20% on brokered deals.

**The inversion was structural, not cosmetic.** The catalog stopped being the engine of the business and became a byproduct of the verification service. Every verification was a potential catalog entry. The chicken-egg loop was broken because the first transaction — the $499 verification — required no buyer, no catalog, and no pre-existing marketplace. Just a production agency with an AI video and a legal team asking questions.

**What the CarFax model clarified about the product:**

The value SI8 provides is not documentation. Documentation will be partially commoditized by C2PA/Content Credentials metadata standards within two to three years. The value is the **judgment layer** — the human legal review that decides whether a particular AI-generated film contains likeness risk, IP exposure, or training data liability that automated metadata cannot assess. A machine can detect a Nike logo in a frame. A machine cannot decide whether that logo constitutes trademark infringement in the context of this specific film, for this specific client, deployed in this specific market.

That judgment is the product. The document is the delivery mechanism.

**The three-gear model that emerged:**

- **Gear A — CaaS Foundation (primary revenue):** $499 per video × 60–70 verifications/year = $30–35K. Immediate cash. No marketplace required.
- **Gear B — Showcase Marketplace (secondary revenue):** 20% commission on licensing deals brokered through the catalog. Grows as Gear A produces verified entries.
- **Gear C — Producer Track (tertiary revenue):** JD curates custom slates for institutional buyers. Uses Gears A and B as infrastructure.

Year 1 revenue target across all three gears: **$80–120K.**

The structure that had been broken through three versions was now sound.

## LinkedIn as a Market Research Instrument

**March–April 2026.**

Version 4 answered the structural question. It created a new one: who, exactly, is the customer?

*"Production agencies who deliver AI video to brand clients with legal teams"* is a direction. It is not an ICP. It doesn't tell you which title to target, which geography to prioritise, which message to lead with, or which industries are worth ignoring. That specificity had to be earned through outreach.

The architecture we built: **three LinkedIn personas** (Ivy, Vanessa, Lilly) operating independently across different audience lists and geographic markets. Every reply — warm, cold, confused, hostile — logged automatically to a Supabase database. A custom Python classifier applied to every response. Not just "did they reply" but "what kind of reply, what does it signal, and what does that tell us about this segment."

Four sales classifications: **warm** (genuine engagement — described pain, asked a question, expressed interest), **pass** (polite no), **NAF** (not a fit — won't use AI, wrong role), **minimal** (too short to classify).

This system wasn't built on day one. It was refined through use. Two classifier bugs found and fixed through real data:

- **The ordering bug:** NAF patterns were checked after pass patterns, causing some definitive wrong-fits to be classified as passes. Fix: check NAF first, always.
- **The negation bug:** "interested" appeared in WARM patterns, which meant "not interested" was being classified as warm. Fix: add a negation override — check for "not interested" before scanning for "interested."

Every bug caught was a false positive eliminated. Every false positive eliminated was a lead that didn't receive a misdirected follow-up.

**Phase 1: Testing v3-era messages on v4 audiences.**

The first campaigns used the framing that had been developed under the v3 model — "trusted AI supplier," process efficiency angles. Results:

| Campaign | Responses | Warm% | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Trusted AI Supplier | 20 | 20% | RETIRE |
| Vetting Takes Weeks | 29 | 14% | RETIRE |

20% warm is not nothing. But the warm replies were asking about production supply — outsourcing video production, not compliance documentation. Wrong buying intent. The message was attracting a different customer than the v4 model needed. These campaigns were retired.

**Phase 2: V4 messaging — Legal Friction vs. Hitting a Wall.**

Two new sequences, built specifically around the compliance pain:

- **Legal Friction:** *"When you deliver AI-generated video to a client — are their legal teams asking for documentation?"* Opens with a question. Surfaces the pain without stating it. Filters for people who recognise the problem immediately.
- **Hitting a Wall:** *"More and more production houses are hitting a wall when client legal teams reject AI video over missing documentation."* States the pain as an industry fact. Reaches people who haven't hit it yet but recognise the risk.

A third variant was also tested: **Blocks AI Campaign** — more aggressive framing, using the word "blocks" to describe what legal teams do. Same pain, higher urgency.

| Campaign | N | Warm% | Pass% | Decision |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Legal Friction | 30 | 33% | 10% | KEEP — strong pre-qualifier |
| Hitting a Wall | 36 | 31% | 19% | SCALE |
| Blocks AI Campaign | 16 | 6% | 31% | KILL |

"Blocks AI Campaign" was killed at 16 responses. The word "blocks" triggered defensiveness rather than recognition. When you imply that someone's legal team is an obstacle, you put them on the defensive before the conversation starts. Data confirmed what the instinct couldn't.

Legal Friction and Hitting a Wall performed nearly identically on warm rate — 33% and 31% — but with different pass profiles. Legal Friction's 10% pass rate (vs. 19% for Hitting a Wall) indicates it's pre-qualifying harder: only people who have already encountered the problem respond warmly. Hitting a Wall reaches an earlier stage of awareness. Both are worth running. They're targeting the same person at different points on the pain curve.

**Phase 3: The audience discovery — one filter that changed everything.**

The most significant finding in the dataset was not about message framing or geography. It was about list construction.

**Creative Director London** — broad targeting — returned a 0% warm rate across 13 responses in the Title × Geography cross-tab. Not low. Zero. After three batches with 8–10% warm at the list level, the segment was formally disqualified.

Meanwhile, a parallel campaign targeting the same geography with one additional filter — only Creative Directors who self-identified with AI video in their LinkedIn profile or posts — returned this:

| Audience List | N | Warm% | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| VP+_Creative_London (broad) | 10–12 per batch | 8–10% | DISQUALIFIED |
| CreaDir_AI Video_England (AI-filtered) | 13 | 38% | PROMISING |

Same message. Same sequence. Same alias. One search filter. **4x improvement in warm rate.**

The reason is not mysterious. A Creative Director who has "AI video" in their LinkedIn profile has already crossed the threshold of *"do I even care about this question?"* before message one arrives. They've committed to AI video as part of their professional identity. They are, by self-selection, pre-qualified on the core premise. The broad Creative Director list includes hundreds of people for whom the AI video compliance question is simply not relevant — they work on live-action, they don't produce AI content, they find the outreach misaligned with their work. The AI-filtered list doesn't include those people.

Audience quality is not a downstream concern. It is the primary variable.

**Geographic validation:**

| Geography | N | Warm% | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Singapore | 26 | 23% | CONFIRMED ✅ |
| London/UK | 67 | 15% | PROMISING 🟡 |

Singapore reached confirmed status — the threshold of n≥15 responses at ≥15% warm rate — faster than any other market. London is one more targeted batch away from the same confirmation. The Singapore result is notable given that the early v3 outreach (~200 contacts, broad targeting) had returned under 5% response rate. The difference between 5% response on broad outreach and 23% warm on filtered outreach is not a different market — it's a different list.

**What warm replies actually said:**

The most useful thing about logged replies is not the classification. It's the language. Warm leads, asked about documentation and legal team friction, described their situation in their own words without prompting:

> *"I would not pitch or work on an AI campaign without clearing this out with the client legal department — it's wasted effort in most cases."*
> — Creative Director & Founder, London

> *"For enterprise level, both legal and compliance teams request documentation on AI-generated content BEFOREHAND, particularly around data sources, rights, applications and how the models are used."*
> — Creative Director, Amsterdam

> *"Since the 2026 ASA and IAB updates, it's become a standard requirement for London projects."*
> — Managing Director, London agency

> *"Great question — I've started seeing this come up more recently, especially with larger clients or agency-side approvals."*
> — Creative Director, London

This language is not constructed. It is not the output of a brand workshop or a copywriter's interpretation of customer pain. It is the exact words a senior agency professional used, unprompted, in a LinkedIn reply, to describe what their working reality looks like. These sentences belong in every SI8 sales conversation, every email sequence, and every website headline.

## The Layer Beneath Sales: Product Intelligence Hidden in "Minimal" Replies

One discovery from the dataset required building an entirely new system to capture.

A reply classified as "minimal" — too short or too vague to indicate buying intent — might contain the most useful product intelligence in the entire dataset.

Example, classified as minimal under the sales classifier:

> *"Of recent yes — before it was never an issue. I think maybe because it's becoming more widely used now."*
> — Creative Director, London

Sales signal: none. This person will not buy in this cycle.

Product signal: they have confirmed the trend, validated the timing, and described the evolution of the market in their own words. This is primary market research that consulting firms charge tens of thousands of dollars to collect.

A secondary boolean classifier — `is_product_feedback()` — was built to run independently of the sales classification. Wide by design: false positives are acceptable. The cost of a false positive is one slightly-off discovery message. The cost of a false negative is missing a senior professional who just described your ICP's exact workflow, for free, in writing.

From 144 total responses, **33 non-warm leads contained product validation signals:**
- 5 NAF (wrong role — but confirming the pain exists in their professional world)
- 4 pass (declined to proceed — but described their current workaround before doing so)
- 24 minimal (no buying intent — but described their process, validated the trend, or asked clarifying questions about what the product actually does)

These 33 leads are not sales leads. They receive a different reply — not a CTA to the $499 package, but a discovery question: *"When you say your legal team reviews it beforehand — what does that documentation actually look like? Is it a standard template or does it vary by project?"*

The answer to that question is worth more than a closed deal in the early stages of a product in a new category.

## The Validated ICP

After 144 responses across multiple message sequences, audience lists, geographies, and alias personas, the ICP that emerged is substantially different from the hypothesis we started with.

**What we thought on day one:** Agencies and production houses who want access to licensed AI film content.

**What the data shows:**

*Creative Directors, AI Content Directors, and Heads of Production at digital agencies and production houses in Singapore and London/UK who are already producing AI video for clients and have encountered — or are anticipating — legal team review of that content. AI-keyword filtering on audience lists is required. Broad title targeting underperforms by 3–5x.*

**What was killed by data:**
- Broad Creative Director targeting in London (8–10% warm across 3 batches — disqualified)
- "Blocks AI Campaign" message variant (6% warm — killed at 16 responses)
- V3 supply-side framing (attracted wrong buyer intent — retired)
- Singapore as a broad-outreach market (200 contacts, <5% response — replaced by filtered targeting at 23% warm)

**What was confirmed:**
- Legal Friction and Hitting a Wall sequences (both >30% warm — keep and scale)
- AI-keyword filtering on Creative Director lists (38% warm vs. 8–10% broad)
- Singapore (CONFIRMED ✅ — 23% warm, n=26)
- London/UK (PROMISING 🟡 — 15% warm, n=67, approaching confirmation)
- The pain is real, self-reported, and described in consistent language across markets

## Five Lessons for B2B Founders Building in Emerging Categories

**1. Your v1 ICP is a hypothesis. Treat it like one.**

The ICP you write on day one is your best current guess. It is not your destination. SI8's original customer — a licensing buyer who wanted access to AI film content — and SI8's v4 customer — a compliance buyer who needs Chain of Title documentation — are different people with different budgets, different decision-making timelines, and different urgency. Expect this. Build outreach infrastructure that generates data, not just leads.

**2. Customer calls are faster than any other validation method.**

Two calls in March 2026 invalidated the v3 model more definitively than three months of outreach had. Get on calls early. Ask for them aggressively. The market will tell you the truth faster, more precisely, and more usefully than any spreadsheet. "Show me what you have" from two buyers in two markets is worth more than 200 LinkedIn responses.

**3. Structural problems cannot be messaged away.**

When the UK agency asked what was in the catalog, the answer was nothing. Writing a better pitch deck would not have changed that answer. Improving the email sequence would not have changed that answer. The business needed a different model — one that generated revenue before the catalog existed — not different words. If your sales process consistently fails at the same point, diagnose the structure before you iterate the message.

**4. Audience quality outperforms message quality.**

The Hitting a Wall message and the Legal Friction message performed nearly identically across multiple batches. The AI-filtered Creative Director list outperformed the broad Creative Director list by 4x. The biggest lever in this dataset was not what we said — it was who we said it to. Before you iterate your copy, audit your list.

**5. Your outreach campaign is a market research instrument.**

Track every reply. Log it. Classify it. Even the polite passes contain intelligence. The 33 product feedback leads in the SI8 dataset validated market timing, described competitor workarounds, confirmed regulatory trends, and provided ICP language — all unprompted, all free. Standard CRM systems throw most of this away. Build the infrastructure to capture it.
