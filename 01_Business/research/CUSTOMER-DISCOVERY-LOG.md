### January 2026 — v1 Model: Supply-Side First

**Signal type:** Outreach result / Internal observation

**Context:**
v1 positioned SI8 as a filmmaker representation agency — supply-side first. The pitch was: sign filmmakers to SI8's roster, build a catalog, then sell licensing deals to platforms, brands, and agencies. Revenue came from distribution commissions (20% of licensing deals).

**What happened:**
- Filmmaker outreach began with IG DMs and cold contact
- Filmmakers showed interest in representation, but the pitch stalled because SI8 had no buyers to show them and no proof the catalog would generate income
- No licensing deals closed under v1

**What we learned:**
- Supply-side first creates a dependency loop: you can't sign meaningful filmmakers without showing them buyers, and you can't show buyers a catalog without signing filmmakers first
- Distribution commission revenue (20% of licensing deals) is too slow for Year 1 — deals take months to close, and you need a catalog before you can close any
- Filmmakers are willing to engage, but they make rational decisions: no proof of income = no commitment

**Product decision:**
→ Pivot to v2. Lead with demand side (brands and agencies) rather than supply side (filmmakers). The buyer-facing pitch opens faster and generates cash before the catalog needs to exist.

### February 2026 — v2 Model: Demand-Side First

**Signal type:** Advisor/peer review + Internal repositioning

**Context:**
v2 reframed the pitch: instead of leading with "we represent filmmakers," lead with "we give brands access to rights-verified AI content that won't get you sued." Revenue model shifted to emphasize buyer-facing services over filmmaker commissions.

**What happened:**
- External peer review (ChatGPT + Gemini) run on v1 model
- Both independently flagged: (1) "rights-cleared" language creates legal liability SI8 can't back up, (2) no geographic focus was a problem, (3) missing credibility assets before outreach
- Language changed throughout: "rights-cleared" → "rights verified" (process, not guarantee)
- Two pricing tiers introduced: Certified Tier (Adobe Firefly only, lowest risk) and Standard Tier (Runway/Pika/Sora)
- Advisory board identified as a credibility asset needed before deals close

**What we learned:**
- "Rights-cleared" implies a legal warranty we can't offer. Language matters. "Rights Verified" signals process rigor without making a guarantee.
- Buyers need credibility signals before engaging: samples, case studies, advisory board names
- Geographic focus matters: trying to hit Singapore + Manila + KL + Bangkok + Jakarta simultaneously = reaching none of them

**Product decision:**
→ v2.1 update: "rights verified" language locked in across all materials. Advisory board added to Month 1 priorities. Singapore selected as first geographic focus before expanding to other SEA markets.

### February–March 2026 — Essa (Hong Kong filmmaker) — Direct conversation

**Signal type:** Direct conversation

**Context:**
Essa was one of the first serious filmmaker prospects approached under the v3 model (Rights Agency + product placement). He creates AI films and was considering distribution representation with SI8.

**What happened:**
- JD pitched the v3 model: submit films to SI8's catalog, SI8 represents and licenses them, filmmaker earns 80% of licensing revenue
- Essa's response: not compelling enough. The 20% royalty share on an unproven platform, with no existing buyer relationships to show, wasn't worth the friction of signing an agreement.
- Paraphrase: "Show me buyers first, then I'll consider it."

**What we learned:**
- Filmmakers think rationally about opportunity cost. An unproven platform + commission structure = low expected value = no action.
- "Show me buyers" is the chicken-egg problem stated plainly: SI8 needs creators to attract buyers, buyers to attract creators. Neither side moves first without proof the other exists.
- The v3 product placement model required creator content as an input — but creators wouldn't supply it without guaranteed demand

**Product decision:**
→ Essa's "show me buyers first" response was one of the two key signals that drove the v3→v4 pivot. The chicken-egg problem was real and needed a structural solution, not better messaging.

### March 2026 — v4 Pivot: Compliance as a Service (CaaS)

**Signal type:** Advisor/peer review + synthesis of creator/buyer signals

**Context:**
After non-conversions from Essa, Leon (creators) and Isaac, The Media Shop (buyers) — all confirming the chicken-egg deadlock — the v3 model was redesigned.

**What happened:**
- Two independent peer reviews (ChatGPT / Gemini) run on the chicken-egg problem and potential solutions
- Both independently converged on the same structural fix: lead with a verification service ($499/video) that generates revenue from Day 1, without needing a pre-existing catalog or buyer relationships
- Key insight from peer review: "Verification customers pay upfront, then opt-in to become marketplace inventory. No separate creator recruitment needed."
- CaaS model named: $499/video SI8 Certified verification → Chain of Title documentation → optional Showcase catalog listing

**What we learned:**
- The chicken-egg problem was structural, not a messaging problem. Fixing it required a different business model, not a better pitch.
- By making the verification service the primary product (not the catalog), SI8 could generate revenue before the marketplace existed — and the marketplace would grow as a byproduct of verifications
- "CarFax for AI video" became the positioning analogy: CarFax makes money from the report (verification), not from car sales (licensing)
- The moat is the judgment layer (human legal review) — not the documentation itself, which will be commoditized by C2PA/Content Credentials

**Product decision:**
→ Full v4 pivot. Three-gear model introduced: Gear A (CaaS verification, $499 primary), Gear B (Showcase marketplace, 20% commission secondary), Gear C (Producer curation, whale buyers tertiary). Platform built from Day 1 to support self-serve scale in Year 3.

### March 30, 2026 — Shingo / shingo4987.film — Direct conversation

**Signal type:** Direct conversation

**Context:**
Shingo is a Japan-based AI filmmaker who responded to SI8's IG outreach. He is active on the AI film festival circuit. His AI short film "AI アイ" just won the Silver AI Performance Award at OMNI International AI Film Festival "AI EMOTION-感動" Shanghai 2026, selected from 500+ entries across Asia. He made the film with @lifeknock.studio in his spare time.

**What happened (exchange 1 — response to outreach):**

- He said he doesn't have a "fully finished AI film that I feel ready to document or put into a catalog"
- He said he's still developing and refining several projects
- He expressed genuine interest in SI8 and said he'd reconnect once he has something ready

**Key observation:** At the time of this message, Shingo had already posted about winning a major AI film festival award — meaning he had a finished, recognized film. Yet his mental model of "ready" didn't include that film.

**What we learned (exchange 1):**
- Creators draw a line between "festival film" and "commercially ready film" — an award-winning piece doesn't automatically feel like something worth documenting or listing
- The reasons for this gap are unknown but worth investigating: Is it about commercial intent? Film quality? Uncertainty about what "documenting" means? Not seeing a licensing path for festival-style work?
- This is a meaningful perception gap: if award-winning films don't feel "ready," what does ready mean to creators? SI8 needs to understand this before it can effectively onboard creators.

**What happened (exchange 2 — follow-up after seeing his OMNI win post):**
- JD congratulated him on the OMNI award
- Asked two market research questions:
  1. Did OMNI or any festivals ask about rights or how the film was made (tools used, training data)?
  2. What would need to be true about a project for him to feel like it's "ready" to document or put in a catalog?
- Awaiting response

**What we hoped to learn:**
- Whether the festival circuit is creating any documentation demand upstream
- What "commercially ready" means to a creator with finished, award-winning work

**Shingo's response (March 30, 2026):**

On festivals:
> "At WAIFF (World AI Film Festival in Japan), they only accept works that comply with their generative AI policy. I also had to disclose all of the tools I used when submitting my film, so there was a clear emphasis on transparency around how the work was made."

On readiness:
> "First, I need to feel confident as a creator that the piece actually expresses what I want to express, both conceptually and aesthetically. From another angle, I also care a lot about the platform itself – its curation philosophy, how it's run, and what kind of track record it has. Those things would definitely influence my decision about where to list a project."

**What we learned:**

1. **Festivals are already creating upstream documentation demand.** WAIFF requires tool disclosure as a submission condition. Creators submitting to AI film festivals are already in disclosure mode — they're the most natural entry point for Creator Record outreach. Festival participants aren't a future audience; they're an active one right now.

2. **"Ready" = creative confidence + platform trust. Not commercial intent.** Shingo's barrier wasn't that the film wasn't finished or that he doesn't want to commercialize. It was that SI8 hadn't yet earned his trust as a platform. "Curation philosophy, how it's run, track record" are what he evaluates before listing. This is a platform credibility problem, not a messaging problem.

3. **Serious creators apply quality filters to platforms.** They're selective about where they list — which is actually good for SI8's long-term positioning (curation = quality signal), but means SI8 needs to communicate its curation standards clearly and build visible track record before serious creators will commit.

**JD's reply:**
> "Super helpful! Thanks and excited to see/follow your work!"

Kept warm, no pressure. Relationship stays open. Shingo is now a warm contact — follow his work and reconnect when he releases something new.

**Product decision:**
→ Two implications: (1) AI film festival participants are a high-fit outreach segment — they're already documenting tools, already thinking about transparency, already in the ecosystem. Future outreach should include festival-adjacent channels (WAIFF, OMNI, etc.). (2) Creator-facing materials need to articulate SI8's curation philosophy more clearly — not just "we verify rights" but "here's how we evaluate what gets listed and why." Platform trust is built through visible evidence of quality curation, not just through the verification product.

## Signal Patterns (updated as entries accumulate)

*Running synthesis — updated periodically, not per-entry.*

**Pattern 1: The chicken-egg deadlock (resolved by v4)**
Both supply and demand sides said the same thing from opposite directions: "show me the other side first." Creators needed proof of buyers; buyers needed proof of catalog. CaaS solved this by making verification (not the catalog) the primary product.
*Sources: Essa, Leon, Isaac, The Media Shop*

**Pattern 2: Creator "readiness" = creative confidence + platform trust**
Creators — even those with finished, award-winning films — don't automatically see their work as ready for commercial documentation. The barrier is not commercial intent or film completion: it's trust in the platform. Serious creators evaluate curation philosophy, track record, and how a platform is run before listing. SI8 needs visible evidence of quality curation to earn that trust.
*Sources: Shingo (March 2026)*

**Pattern 6: Festivals are already asking for tool disclosure**
AI film festivals (WAIFF confirmed) require tool disclosure as a submission condition. Creators entering festival circuits are already building documentation habits around transparency. This is an upstream channel SI8 hasn't yet tapped — festival participants are a high-fit, already-primed audience for Creator Record.
*Sources: Shingo / WAIFF (March 2026)*

**Pattern 3: Regulatory anxiety is real and growing**
European and UK markets showed stronger response to the compliance framing than Singapore/SEA. EU AI Act awareness, TikTok enforcement failures, Facebook banned AI ads — the regulatory pressure is creating real anxiety in the agency market. SI8's compliance positioning is well-timed.
*Sources: London outreach response rate, news digest signals (Mar 30, 2026)*

**Pattern 4: Distribution demand exists but has no infrastructure**
Audiences actively look for full AI films when they see festival clips. Creators have no place to send them. This is a future Gear B opportunity.
*Sources: OMNI festival audience comments (Mar 2026)*

**Pattern 5: Free + education unlocks the conversation**
Comp codes (100% off Creator Record) significantly reduce friction for initial engagement. Creators need substantial context before evaluating the offer — the educational layer is part of the product.
*Sources: keeper505, Shingo exchanges (Mar 2026)*
