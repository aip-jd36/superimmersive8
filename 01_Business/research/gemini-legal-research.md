## Section 1: Damage Mitigation Evidence

### Finding #1: Innocent Infringer Defense Reduces Statutory Damages by 73%+

**Source:** 17 U.S.C. § 504(c)(2)

**Date:** Current Statute

**Key Facts:** Standard statutory damages for copyright infringement range from $750 to $30,000 per work. If a defendant proves they "was not aware and had no reason to believe that his or her acts constituted an infringement of copyright," the court may reduce damages to $200. Willful infringement raises the cap to $150,000.

**Direct Quote:** "In a case where the infringer sustains the burden of proving, and the court finds, that such infringer was not aware and had no reason to believe that his or her acts constituted an infringement of copyright, the court in its discretion may reduce the award of statutory damages to a sum of not less than $200."

**Relevance to SI8:** Proving "no reason to believe" requires documented good-faith reliance on clearances. SI8's Field #4 (Commercial Use Authorization) and Field #3 (Rights Verified Sign-off) provide the exact evidentiary paper trail a brand needs to trigger the statutory reduction.

**Quantifiable Impact:** Reduces minimum damages by 73% (from $750 to $200) and maximum exposure by up to 99% per work (preventing the $150,000 willful multiplier).

## Section 2: Vicarious & Contributory Liability Protection

### Finding #2: Secondary Liability Requires "Knowledge" and "Control"

**Source:** Foundational IP Law (e.g., Inwood Labs v. Ives Labs) and ongoing cases like Warner Bros. v. Midjourney (2025)

**Date:** 2025-2026 litigation

**Key Facts:** Brands commissioning content can be sued for secondary (vicarious or contributory) infringement if they have the "right and ability to supervise" the creator or have "knowledge of specific infringers."

**Relevance to SI8:** If a brand blindly commissions an agency to use AI, they are vicariously exposed to the agency's bad prompts. SI8's Field #1 (Tool Provenance Log) and our documented "No List" act as a contractual shield. It legally proves the brand did not authorize infringing behavior, breaking the "knowledge and contribution" chain of liability.

## Section 3: Regulatory Compliance Requirements

### Finding #3: Colorado AI Act Grants "Rebuttable Presumption" for Documentation

**Source:** Colorado Artificial Intelligence Act (SB24-205)

**Date:** Passed May 2024, Effective 2026

**Key Facts:** The Act imposes a duty of "reasonable care" on deployers of high-risk AI systems. Crucially, the law explicitly states that entities maintaining strict documentation (training data summaries, evaluation logs, risk mitigation) are granted a legal safe harbor.

**Direct Quote:** "Developers and deployers maintain a rebuttable presumption of using reasonable care under this provision if they satisfy the obligations of the Act... [including] documentation describing how the system was evaluated... and data governance measures."

**Relevance to SI8:** SI8's Chain of Title acts exactly like the required "evaluation & mitigation" compliance packet. Field #2 (Model Disclosure) and Field #6 (Category Conflict Log) satisfy the "reasonable care" documentation standard.

## Section 5: Fair Use & Copyrightability

### Finding #6: US Copyright Office Rejects "Prompts" as Authorship

**Source:** U.S. Copyright Office: Copyright and Artificial Intelligence, Part 2: Copyrightability Report

**Date:** January 2025

**Key Facts:** The USCO explicitly stated that entering a text prompt into an AI generator does not equal human authorship. To gain copyright protection (which brands require to own their ads), there must be documented "expressive inputs" and human editorial control.

**Direct Quote:** "Given current generally available technology, prompts alone do not provide sufficient human control to make users of an AI system the authors of the output... However, [in cases of substantial modification or expressive input] the human's own creative expression will be protected by copyright."

**Relevance to SI8:** This completely validates Field #9 (Version History) and your requirement that filmmakers submit a 150-word Human Authorship Declaration detailing their editorial choices, cuts, and modifications. Without this, the brand literally cannot own the copyright to the commercial.
