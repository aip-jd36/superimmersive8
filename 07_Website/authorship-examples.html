Submission Guidance

Authorship Declaration Examples

Examples showing how to describe your creative process for the Rights Verified submission. Choose the level of detail that fits your workflow.

Why This Matters

The U.S. Copyright Office requires evidence of human creative control over AI-generated content for copyright protection. Your authorship declaration demonstrates:

  • Prompt authorship: The creative decisions you made in crafting prompts
  • Iteration & selection: How you refined outputs through multiple attempts
  • Editorial choices: What you kept, discarded, or modified
  • Post-production: Human editing, assembly, and creative direction

Minimum requirement: 150 words describing your creative process in detail.

Example 1: Lightweight (Acceptable)

~175 words Minimal but acceptable

Project: "Neon Solitude" — A cyberpunk cityscape film

Tool: Runway Gen-3 Alpha Turbo

I created "Neon Solitude" using Runway Gen-3 Alpha Turbo with a series of carefully crafted prompts focused on cyberpunk aesthetics and urban isolation. My initial prompt described a lone figure walking through rain-soaked neon streets, but the first outputs were too generic. I iterated by adding specific details: "volumetric fog," "pink and blue neon reflections on wet pavement," and "shallow depth of field focusing on the figure's silhouette."

I generated about 25 variations, selecting the 8 clips that best captured the mood I envisioned. I discarded outputs where the neon colors felt too saturated or the figure's movement looked unnatural. In post-production, I assembled the selected clips in DaVinci Resolve, added color grading to enhance the blue-pink contrast, and composed an original ambient score using Logic Pro. The final cut reflects my creative vision of isolation in a hyperconnected future city.

Example 2: Medium (Good Detail)

~280 words Strong evidence of authorship

Project: "The Last Garden" — A solarpunk narrative short

Tools: Sora + Runway Gen-3 + ElevenLabs + Adobe Premiere

I conceived "The Last Garden" as a solarpunk narrative about a botanist preserving Earth's final greenhouse during ecological collapse. The creative process involved multiple AI tools and significant human direction at every stage.

Pre-visualization: I storyboarded 12 key scenes by hand, defining the emotional arc and visual progression from despair (abandoned cities) to hope (thriving greenhouse).

Generation: I used Sora for wide environmental shots and Runway Gen-3 for character close-ups. For the greenhouse scenes, my prompts specified "soft natural light filtering through overgrown vines, warm earth tones, shallow focus on hands planting seeds." I generated over 60 clips, iterating on lighting direction, camera movement, and color palette. I rejected outputs where the greenhouse felt sterile or the character's expressions looked detached.

Selection criteria: I chose clips based on three factors: (1) visual consistency with my storyboard, (2) natural-looking motion and emotion, and (3) color harmony across scenes. About 40% of generated clips were discarded.

Post-production: In Adobe Premiere, I assembled 18 final clips into a 5-minute narrative arc, adding cross-dissolves to suggest time passing. I recorded voiceover narration (the botanist's journal entries) using ElevenLabs, then edited it for pacing and emotion. I composed ambient music in Ableton Live and mixed all audio elements for a cohesive soundscape. The final film reflects my creative vision of hope and resilience in climate collapse.

Example 3: Detailed (Comprehensive)

~420 words Exceptionally thorough

Project: "Echoes of Tomorrow" — A sci-fi experimental film

Tools: Runway Gen-3 + Kling AI + Midjourney + ElevenLabs + DaVinci Resolve + Logic Pro

I created "Echoes of Tomorrow" as a 7-minute experimental sci-fi film exploring memory fragmentation in a post-human society. The project involved extensive creative direction across multiple AI tools and traditional editing workflows.

Concept development: I began with a 3-page treatment outlining the narrative structure, visual motifs (mirrored surfaces, fractured reflections, desaturated color), and emotional beats. I created a mood board in Figma with reference images for lighting, composition, and atmosphere.

Pre-visualization: Using Midjourney, I generated 50+ concept frames to establish the visual language. I selected 12 key frames that captured the aesthetic I wanted—clinical white environments contrasted with warm memory fragments. These became my reference images for video generation.

Video generation workflow: I used Runway Gen-3 for photorealistic scenes (sterile lab environments, close-ups of technology) and Kling AI for dreamlike memory sequences. My prompts were highly detailed, specifying camera movement, lighting ratios, depth of field, and emotional tone. For example, the opening scene prompt: "Static shot, clinical white lab, single figure in medical gown facing a mirrored wall, soft overhead lighting creating subtle shadows, shallow focus on reflection, 4K photorealistic, cold blue color grade."

Iteration process: I generated 120+ clips total, running 3-5 variations per scene with adjusted parameters (camera angle, lighting intensity, movement speed). I maintained a spreadsheet tracking each generation with notes on what worked and what to adjust. Roughly 50% of outputs were rejected for issues like inconsistent lighting, unnatural motion, or aesthetic mismatch with my vision.

Selection and editorial decisions: From 120 clips, I selected 28 that formed a cohesive visual narrative. I made deliberate choices to create contrast: fast cuts in fragmented memory sequences vs. long static takes in lab scenes. I discarded clips that felt too polished or emotionally detached.

Post-production: In DaVinci Resolve, I color graded all footage to emphasize the cold/warm dichotomy—desaturated blues for present-day scenes, warm amber tones for memories. I added glitch effects and frame blending to reinforce the theme of degraded memory. I recorded custom voiceover using ElevenLabs (synthesized voice representing the AI narrator), then edited and layered it with ambient sound design created in Logic Pro using field recordings I processed and manipulated. Final audio mix took 12 hours to balance dialog, sound design, and the minimalist score I composed.

The entire creative process from concept to final cut took 6 weeks. Every decision—from prompt wording to clip selection to color grading—was guided by my creative vision of memory and identity in a technological future.

What To Avoid

These examples would not meet the authorship requirement:

❌ Too generic:

"I used Runway to create a sci-fi video. I wrote prompts describing what I wanted and selected the best outputs. Then I edited them together in Premiere Pro."

Why it fails: No specific creative decisions, no iteration process, no evidence of human artistic control.

❌ Just technical specs:

"I generated 50 clips using Sora with the following prompts: [list of prompts]. I used 1080p resolution, 24fps, and exported in H.264."

Why it fails: Lists technical details but doesn't describe creative decisions, selection criteria, or artistic intent.

❌ AI made the decisions:

"I let the AI generate various options and used the ones that looked best. The AI did most of the creative work."

Why it fails: Suggests AI, not human, made creative choices. This undermines copyright protection.

Quick Checklist

Your authorship declaration should answer these questions:

  • ✓ What was your creative vision or concept for the film?
  • ✓ What specific prompts did you write, and why those particular descriptions?
  • ✓ How many variations did you generate, and what were you testing?
  • ✓ What criteria did you use to select outputs (composition, emotion, aesthetics)?
  • ✓ What did you reject, and why?
  • ✓ What post-production editing did you do (assembly, color, audio, effects)?
  • ✓ How did your creative decisions shape the final film?
