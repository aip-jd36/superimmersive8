# Getty/Shutterstock Agreement Structure Research

**Date:** March 18, 2026
**Purpose:** Understand how stock platforms separate contributor and customer agreements
**Sources:** Getty Images Contributor Agreement v5.1, iStock Artist Supply Agreement, Shutterstock Contributor TOS 2023

---

## Key Finding: Two Separate Contracts, Zero Overlap

Getty, iStock, and Shutterstock all operate with **completely separate agreements** for contributors vs. customers:

| Agreement Type | Parties | What It Governs | What Contributors Receive |
|----------------|---------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| **Contributor Agreement** | Platform ↔ Contributor | Rights granted to platform, royalty rates, what contributor can/can't do | Royalty reports, sales data, account access |
| **Customer License Agreement** | Platform ↔ Buyer | Usage rights, restrictions, indemnification, liability limits | N/A - contributors are not parties to this contract |

**Critical insight:** Contributors never receive copies of customer license agreements, and this is never explicitly prohibited - it's simply not mentioned as something contributors get.

---

## What Contributors Actually Receive

### iStock Agreement (Section 5.d):
> "iStock will use commercially reasonable efforts to provide real time reporting of downloads of Accepted Content from the iStock Site and gettyimages.com for your information. **Please note that the official report of licenses of Accepted Content and corresponding Royalties shall be the Sales Report.**"

**Contributors get:**
- Sales reports (licenses granted, dates, revenue)
- Royalty amounts
- Download/license counts
- Buyer industry (sometimes)

**Contributors DON'T get:**
- Copies of customer license agreements
- Buyer contact information
- License terms negotiated with specific buyers
- Platform's indemnification terms with buyers

---

### Getty Images FAQ:
> "You will receive a detailed statement of licenses granted in the previous month showing the royalties that are due to you."

**Statement of licenses ≠ License agreements**

---

### Shutterstock TOS (Section 8):
> "Royalty payments and accompanying statements will be issued monthly, on or about the 15th day of each month, for the previous month's downloads."

Again: **Statements**, not license documents.

## Why There's No Explicit Prohibition

**In 20-30 page legal agreements that specify:**
- What contributors receive (royalty reports, payment schedules, account access)
- What contributors can do (self-promotion, limited edition prints)
- What contributors can't do (submit to other agencies, license content directly, share login credentials)

**Customer license agreements are NEVER mentioned.**

**The absence = the policy.**

If contributors were meant to receive customer license agreements, these detailed contracts would say so.

## How Platforms Keep the Two Sides Separate

### 1. Getty Images

**Contributor Agreement covers:**
- Section 3: Grant of Authority to license content
- Section 5: Compensation (royalty rates, payment terms)
- Section 7: Credit and attribution (when required)
- Section 9: Representations and warranties (contributor's obligations)
- Section 10: Indemnity (contributor protects Getty from claims)

**Customer License Agreement (separate document) covers:**
- What customers can/can't do with content
- Indemnification (Getty protects customers from claims)
- Liability limits (caps on Getty's exposure)
- License types (RF, RM, RR)

**No crossover.** Each party sees only their own agreement.

---

### 2. iStock

**Contributor sees:**
- Royalty rates in Rate Card (incorporated by reference)
- Sales reports showing: date, content ID, license type, royalty amount
- Account dashboard showing downloads

**Customer sees:**
- Customer License Agreement (separate)
- Download confirmation
- License terms for their specific use

**iStock controls both relationships separately.**

---

### 3. Shutterstock

**Contributor Agreement Section 1.c:**
> "Shutterstock shall have the right, but not the obligation, to license all Content through any of Shutterstock's brands and platforms to its customers for use in perpetuity **in accordance with license agreements entered into by Shutterstock**"

**Key phrase:** "license agreements entered into by Shutterstock"

This makes clear:
- Shutterstock enters into license agreements with customers
- Contributors are not parties to those agreements
- Contributors grant Shutterstock the authority to do this

## What Keeps Contributors in the Dark About Customer Terms?

### Information Contributors Receive:

**From all three platforms:**
1. **Sales/royalty reports showing:**
   - Date of license
   - Content ID
   - License type (RF, RM, Standard, Enhanced, etc.)
   - Revenue amount
   - Royalty earned

2. **Account dashboards showing:**
   - Total downloads
   - Total earnings
   - Performance stats

3. **General statements like:**
   - "Your content was licensed for commercial use"
   - "Licensed for web/broadcast/merchandise" (sometimes)

### Information Contributors DON'T Receive:

1. **Specific buyer details:**
   - Buyer company name (usually)
   - Buyer contact information (never)
   - Buyer's intended use (detailed description)

2. **License agreement terms:**
   - What specific rights were granted
   - Geographic restrictions (if any)
   - Duration of license
   - Exclusivity terms
   - Indemnification caps
   - Liability limits

3. **Pricing details:**
   - What buyer actually paid (vs. what contributor earned)
   - Whether any discounts were applied
   - Whether buyer purchased additional rights/services

## Getty's Credit Attribution Example

**Getty Customer License Agreement:**
> "The use of Images and Video in connection with news reporting, commentary, publishing, or any other 'editorial' context, shall be accompanied by an adjacent credit to the Shutterstock contributor and to Shutterstock in substantially the following form: 'Name of Artist/Shutterstock.com'"

**Getty Contributor Agreement:**
> "Using the name identified by you in your contributor account profile, iStock shall use commercially reasonable efforts to credit you as the source of Accepted Content, **but shall have no liability for lack of credit.**"

**Notice:**
- Customer agreement specifies when credit is required
- Contributor agreement says "we'll try but no guarantees"
- Contributor never sees the customer agreement requiring credit
- If customer fails to credit, contributor has no recourse against customer (only against platform, and platform has limited liability)

**This asymmetry is intentional and protects the platform.**

---

## Why This Structure Protects the Platform

### 1. Prevents Direct Negotiation
If contributors saw customer license terms, they might:
- Try to negotiate directly with buyers (bypassing platform)
- Demand higher royalties based on what buyers are paying
- Object to specific license terms they disagree with

### 2. Protects Pricing Strategy
If contributors knew:
- Getty charges buyer $3,000 for RM license
- Contributor gets $600 (20%)
- Getty keeps $2,400 (80%)

Contributors might:
- Demand higher royalty rates
- Feel terms are unfair
- Leave platform for better deals

### 3. Prevents Liability Exposure
If contributors saw:
- Getty's indemnification terms with buyers
- Getty's liability caps ($10K, $250K, etc.)
- Getty's warranty disclaimers

Contributors might:
- Realize platform is shifting risk to them via contributor indemnification
- Demand matching liability protection
- Seek legal advice and push back on terms

## Key Quotes from Contributor Agreements

### Getty Images (Section 3.a.ii):
> "The right to grant perpetual, worldwide, licenses or sublicenses to end-users. **iStock and its Distribution Partners will determine the terms and conditions of all licenses of Content granted by them**, but will not use or license Content for uses that are defamatory, pornographic or otherwise illegal."

**Translation:** Platform controls customer license terms entirely. Contributor has no say.

---

### Shutterstock (Section 1.a):
> "You grant to Shutterstock a worldwide, sublicensable, non-exclusive right and license to index, analyze, categorize, archive reproduce, prepare derivative works incorporating, publicly display, **sell**, advertise and market, any Content uploaded by you and accepted by Shutterstock **for any reasonable business purpose**"

**Translation:** Shutterstock can sell content on any terms it deems reasonable. Contributor grants this authority upfront.

---

### iStock (Section 3.a.i):
> "The worldwide right to market and sublicense the right to copy, use, reproduce, distribute, redistribute, sublicense, publish, republish, upload, post, transmit, broadcast, crop, modify, alter, create derivative works of, package, repackage, produce and sell prints or similar image products, or publicly perform or display Content **to prospective licensees** in any and all media now in existence or that may in the future be introduced"

**Translation:** iStock controls how content is marketed and what licenses are offered. Contributor is not involved in those decisions.

## What SI8 Should Learn From This

### 1. Two Separate Agreements, Zero Crossover

**Filmmaker Agreement should cover:**
- Rights granted to SI8
- Royalty rates and payment terms
- What filmmaker can/can't do with their content
- What filmmaker receives (approval status, earnings reports)

**Buyer License Agreement should cover:**
- Rights granted to buyer
- Usage restrictions
- Indemnification and liability
- What buyer receives (video file + Chain of Title package)

**Filmmaker never sees buyer agreement. Buyer never sees filmmaker agreement.**

---

### 2. Specify What Filmmaker DOES Get (Not What They Don't)

**In Filmmaker Agreement:**

```
RIGHTS VERIFIED APPROVAL & REPORTING

Upon approval, Creator will receive:

a. Rights Verified Approval confirmation via email
b. Catalog ID (e.g., SI8-2026-0003)
c. Catalog listing at superimmersive8.com/catalog
d. Access to Creator Dashboard showing:
   - Active catalog entries
   - License activity (date, license type, revenue)
   - Monthly earnings statements
   - Cumulative royalties earned
   - Buyer industry segment (not buyer name)

Creator Dashboard is updated monthly. Detailed earnings statements
are provided on or before the 15th of each month for the previous
month's licenses.
```

**Notice:** No mention of receiving buyer license agreements. Just like Getty/Shutterstock.

---

### 3. Filmmaker Agreement: Control Over Licensing Terms

**In Filmmaker Agreement:**

```
LICENSING AUTHORITY

Creator hereby grants SI8 the exclusive right to:

a. Determine pricing, license terms, and conditions for all licenses
   of Accepted Content;
b. Enter into license agreements with buyers on Creator's behalf;
c. Modify license terms to accommodate buyer requirements, provided
   such modifications do not exceed the rights granted herein;
d. Grant perpetual, worldwide licenses to end users in accordance
   with SI8's standard license terms.

SI8 will not license Accepted Content for uses that are pornographic,
defamatory, or otherwise illegal.

Creator acknowledges that SI8 and its buyers will determine the final
terms and conditions of all licenses relating to Accepted Content.
```

**This language (adapted from Getty) gives SI8 full control over customer licensing.**

---

### 4. Two-Way Information Flow (But Asymmetric)

**What SI8 tells filmmaker:**
- "Your work was licensed on [date]"
- "License type: Standard / Extended / Custom Placement"
- "You earned $X (Y% royalty)"
- "Buyer industry: Advertising Agency / CPG Brand / Streaming Platform"

**What SI8 tells buyer:**
- "This content passed Rights Verified review"
- "Chain of Title documentation included"
- "Licensed for [specific use terms in agreement]"
- "Indemnification up to $[limit]"

**What filmmaker NEVER sees:**
- What buyer paid (unless 1:1 with filmmaker's royalty rate)
- Specific buyer name and contact info
- Full license terms granted to buyer
- Indemnification terms between SI8 and buyer
- Liability caps

**What buyer NEVER sees:**
- Filmmaker's name (unless credited)
- Filmmaker's contact information
- Filmmaker's royalty rate
- How much SI8 paid filmmaker

---

### 5. Indemnification Structure

**Filmmaker Agreement (contributor indemnifies platform):**

```
INDEMNIFICATION

Creator agrees to indemnify, defend, and hold harmless SI8 and its
officers, directors, employees, and customers from any claims, damages,
or expenses arising from:

a. Breach of Creator's representations and warranties herein;
b. Any claim that Accepted Content infringes third-party rights;
c. Inaccurate or falsified model/property releases;
d. Use of Accepted Content within the scope of licenses granted by SI8.

SI8 will notify Creator promptly of any such claims and will have the
right to control defense of such claims.
```

**Buyer License Agreement (platform indemnifies buyer):**

```
INDEMNIFICATION

SI8 agrees to indemnify Buyer against third-party claims that Buyer's
use of Content in accordance with this license infringes third-party
copyrights or violates rights of privacy/publicity, subject to:

a. SI8's maximum liability of $[amount] per asset;
b. Buyer's prompt notification of any claims;
c. SI8's right to control defense of such claims;
d. Exclusions for buyer modifications or misuse.
```

**Notice:**
- Filmmaker protects SI8 from claims (unlimited exposure)
- SI8 protects buyer from claims (capped exposure)
- Filmmaker never sees buyer indemnification terms
- Asymmetric risk distribution

## SI8 Implementation: What to Create

### 1. Filmmaker Agreement Section: "What You Receive"

```
RIGHTS VERIFIED APPROVAL & CREATOR DASHBOARD

Upon Rights Verified approval, Creator receives:

1. Email confirmation of approval with:
   - Catalog ID (e.g., SI8-2026-0003)
   - Tier assignment (Tier 1, Tier 1+2)
   - Any restrictions or conditions

2. Catalog listing at superimmersive8.com/catalog with:
   - Creator credit (name/pseudonym)
   - Work title and thumbnail
   - "Rights Verified ✓" badge
   - Tier availability indicators

3. Creator Dashboard access showing:
   - All approved works in catalog
   - License activity: date, type, revenue
   - Monthly earnings statements
   - Cumulative royalties
   - Buyer industry (not company name)

Monthly statements are provided on or before the 15th of each month
for licenses granted in the previous month. Royalty payments are
made within 30 days of statement date, subject to minimum payout
threshold of $100.

SI8 will use commercially reasonable efforts to provide accurate
reporting, but Creator's sole remedy for reporting errors is
correction and adjustment of future payments.

Creator is not entitled to, and will not receive, copies of
license agreements between SI8 and buyers. All licensing terms,
pricing, and buyer relationships are managed by SI8 in accordance
with Section [Licensing Authority].
```

**Key elements:**
- ✅ Specifies exactly what filmmaker gets
- ✅ Explicitly states filmmaker does NOT get buyer license agreements
- ✅ Mirrors Getty/Shutterstock model
- ✅ Protects SI8's buyer relationship

---

### 2. Buyer License Agreement: No Mention of Filmmaker

```
LICENSE AGREEMENT

This agreement is between [Buyer Name] ("Licensee") and
SuperImmersive8 ("SI8").

1. LICENSED CONTENT
   SI8 grants Licensee a [Standard/Extended/Custom] license to
   use the video content identified as [Catalog ID] (the "Content").

2. RIGHTS GRANTED
   [Standard license terms - usage rights, restrictions, etc.]

3. CHAIN OF TITLE DOCUMENTATION
   SI8 provides Licensee with Chain of Title documentation showing:
   - AI tools used and commercial plan verification
   - Model/property release status
   - Rights Verified approval confirmation
   - Commercial use authorization

4. REPRESENTATIONS AND WARRANTIES
   SI8 represents that:
   a. SI8 has the authority to grant this license
   b. Content does not infringe third-party copyrights
   c. Content passed Rights Verified vetting process

5. INDEMNIFICATION
   SI8 will indemnify Licensee against third-party copyright
   infringement claims up to $[amount], subject to [conditions].

6. ATTRIBUTION
   Attribution to creator is optional. If provided, use format:
   "[Work Title] / SuperImmersive8 Rights Verified Catalog"

   Creator name and contact information are not disclosed to
   Licensee. All inquiries regarding Content must be directed
   to SI8.
```

**Key elements:**
- ✅ Agreement is between SI8 and buyer only
- ✅ No filmmaker name or contact info disclosed
- ✅ All rights granted by SI8 (not filmmaker)
- ✅ Attribution is optional and doesn't identify filmmaker by default
- ✅ Filmmaker is never mentioned as a party

## Why This Structure Benefits SI8

### 1. Prevents Disintermediation
If filmmaker had buyer contact info + license terms + Chain of Title, they could pitch buyers directly in the future.

**With separation:**
- Filmmaker doesn't know which brands license their work
- Buyer doesn't know how to reach filmmaker directly
- SI8 controls both relationships

---

### 2. Protects Pricing Strategy
If filmmaker knew:
- Buyer paid $3,000 for Standard License
- Filmmaker got $1,500 (50% Year 1 rate)
- SI8 kept $1,500

Filmmaker might:
- Demand higher split
- Question SI8's value vs. 50% commission
- Try to negotiate directly with buyer at $2,500 (still cheaper for buyer, more for filmmaker)

**With separation:**
- Filmmaker only knows: "I earned $1,500"
- Doesn't know if buyer paid $3,000 or $2,000 or $5,000
- Can't calculate SI8's margin

---

### 3. Protects Liability Structure
If filmmaker saw SI8's indemnification terms with buyers:
- "SI8 indemnifies buyer up to $25,000 per asset"
- "SI8 warrants content does not infringe third-party rights"

Filmmaker might realize:
- SI8 is giving buyers more protection than filmmaker is getting
- Filmmaker bears unlimited liability
- SI8 caps its exposure to buyers at $25K but has unlimited recovery from filmmaker

**With separation:**
- Filmmaker knows: "I indemnify SI8 for any claims"
- Doesn't know: How much SI8 commits to buyers
- Can't compare the asymmetry

## Precedent: Getty's Opaque Model

**From earlier research note:**
> "Getty doesn't disclose photographer names in license docs (just asset ID)"

**This means:**
- Buyer receives: "Asset ID: 1234567890"
- Buyer doesn't receive: Photographer name, contact info, or contributor terms
- Photographer receives: "Your asset was licensed [date] for $X"
- Photographer doesn't receive: Buyer name, buyer's license terms, total price paid

**Both sides are deliberately kept separate.**

**SI8 should adopt the same model:**
- Buyer receives: Catalog ID (SI8-2026-0003), Chain of Title docs, license agreement
- Buyer doesn't receive: Filmmaker name (unless credited), filmmaker contact info, filmmaker's royalty rate
- Filmmaker receives: Approval status, earnings report, license count
- Filmmaker doesn't receive: Buyer identity, buyer's license terms, Chain of Title package

## Conclusion

**Getty, iStock, and Shutterstock all operate the same way:**

1. **Two separate agreements** (contributor vs. customer)
2. **No crossover** (contributors don't see customer license terms)
3. **No explicit prohibition** (it's just not mentioned as something contributors get)
4. **Information asymmetry is intentional** (protects platform's value layer)

**SI8 should adopt this exact model:**
- Filmmaker agreement specifies what they receive (approval + earnings)
- Buyer agreement is separate (filmmaker not a party)
- Chain of Title is delivered to buyers only
- Both sides controlled by SI8, neither has direct contact

**This protects SI8's value layer and prevents disintermediation.**
